Strategy

The Attack-Defense Spectrum

When it comes to attack and defense, both players are always in opposing roles. If one player is attacking, the other must be defending. However, many situations are neutral, with neither player having a clear advantage. In these cases, one player may be slightly more on the offensive side of the spectrum, while the other is slightly more defensive. The later and lower you take the shuttle, the earlier and higher your opponent can take it. The more pressure you are under, the more comfortable the situation becomes for your opponent.

So what does this mean for your strategy?

When defending, it is wise to be patient and first neutralize the situation rather than attempting to hit a winning shot immediately. Once you gain the attack, you should aim to maintain it by playing fast downward shots, attacking clears, and flat lifts or pushes. When an opportunity arises, you can then try to finish the rally with a winning shot*.

Side note: Even if you do not go for a winning shot from the rear court, maintaining pressure will often lead to a situation where your opponent can no longer play effectively to the rear court and can only return the shuttle weakly over the net, allowing you to finish the rally at the net.

The goal of any shot generally falls into one of two categories:

  • to put your opponent under pressure
  • to avoid being put under pressure by your opponent

At first glance, these may seem similar. After all, if your opponent is under pressure, you are less likely to be under pressure yourself.

However, these two approaches are fundamentally different and form the basis of effective strategy.

When defending, your goal is to limit your opponent's attacking options by playing a neutral (or neutralizing) shot. Such a shot is unlikely to win the rally immediately, but it prevents your opponent from applying further pressure. This allows you to stay in the rally and regain a more balanced position.

When and how to play neutralizing shots is explained further below.

There are also counter-attacking situations, where you transition directly from defense to attack without neutral play. This can include guiding a weak smash into open space, playing over an opponent who is rushing the net, or using unexpected cross-court shots.

Shot Selection

Applying Pressure

A single shot can put your opponent under pressure or win you a point in several ways:

  • pace
  • precision
  • deception

Ideally, a shot would excel in all three qualities, but in practice this is rarely the case. Often, prioritizing one aspect comes at the expense of another.

For example, when playing a net shot, you can try to place it very tight to the net. Alternatively, you can disguise it as a lift before playing the net shot, which may slightly reduce your ability to place it with maximum precision.

The point is to mix it up. If you always hit the shuttle as early as possible and never delay your shots, your play becomes predictable and your opponent will adjust. On the other hand, if every shot is a deception, the effect wears off, as your opponent will simply wait.

Side note: Even when delaying a shot, you should still reach the shuttle as early as possible with both your body and racket prepared, giving yourself the option to either hit early or delay the shot to create deception. The threat of the early shot remains, forcing your opponent to stay ready for it and making the delay more effective.

At the same time, your shots should generally be disguised in a consistent way. The distinction between active deception and overall disguise is explained in the next subchapter.

Variation does not have to be random. For instance, you might play a deceptive drop shot several times, and just as your opponent begins to expect it, you switch to a smash. Before your opponent starts anticipating the smash, you can introduce a precise and steep half-smash or a delayed attacking clear. This is one way to keep your opponent guessing and ensure that pace, precision, and deception are used to their full potential.

Disguise

Disguise can take two forms.

In general, shots should be disguised by concealing your intention through consistent body movement and hitting action. This means making different shots from the same hitting position look the same until the very last moment before the racket makes contact with the shuttle. This prevents your opponent from reading your shots early, reducing their reaction time.

The second form of disguise involves occasionally faking one shot while executing another. The deviation from your usual movement should be subtle—barely noticeable to your opponent, so it does not become obvious.

Drop Shots

In singles, very slow drop shots are rare. Instead, drop shots exist on a spectrum from fast to slightly slower. However, there is a limit to how slow they should be. Even drop shots on the slow end of that spectrum should still be fast enough to land around the front service line; anything slower will usually let your opponent reach the shuttle too early.

Besides moving your opponent and forcing them out of position, the goal of a drop shot is to maintain an offensive advantage on the reply, whether the reply is played to the front or rear court. A drop shot that is too slow may still move your opponent, but without applying pressure, often leaving you in a defensive situation.

Slower drops are mainly useful when your opponent is already out of position or when they play a short lift or clear.

The closer you are to the baseline when hitting, the faster your drop shots should be.

When playing drops to your non-racket side, you can slice the shuttle; drops to your racket side can be played using a reverse slice. The purpose of slicing and reverse slicing is to disguise the shot direction and make the shot appear faster than it actually is—the hitting action can resemble a smash.

Smashes

There are several variations of the smash:

  • smash to the sidelines
  • smash toward the body
  • steep and precise smash played without full power
  • stick smash (mainly using the wrist and minimal arm movement)
  • half-smash (“tactical smash”)
  • flat smash to the rear court

When should you play each of these shots?

The best situation to play a full smash is when your opponent's lift or clear is slightly short and they are out of position. Sometimes only one of these two conditions is met, and it may still make sense to play a full smash—for example, if the lift is particularly short or your opponent's positioning is especially poor. However, if the lift is deep and your opponent is well positioned, a full-power smash is usually not advisable, as it can be countered.

If the lift or clear is high and reaches the baseline, it may be wiser to play a half-smash instead of a full smash.

If you can intercept a flat lift, you can use a stick smash. This is especially useful when the situation is otherwise favorable for smashing, but you have limited time to execute a full swing.

As alternatives to a full smash to the sidelines, you can play a smash toward your opponent's body, which can be difficult to return, or a flat smash to the rear court. A flat smash to the rear court should be played down the line rather than cross-court. This can be useful if your opponent is still recovering from a cross-court shot and is expecting a steeper smash they may have to dive for.

Shot Options from the Baseline

Building on the discussion of smashes and drops above, your main shot options from the baseline are:

  • fast drop shot
  • half-smash
  • clear

Neutral Shots

A neutral shot is played either in response to another neutral shot or to neutralize a defensive situation. There are two types of neutral shots:

Flat neutral shots

The first type of neutral shot is a controlled, fast, and flat shot over the net that travels far enough into the opponent's court to prevent any net kill or tight, tumbling net shot (aka "spinning" net shot) by the opponent. It can be soft and similar in trajectory to a short serve, or slightly faster. If taken at net height in the middle or rear court, it can be even faster, essentially becoming a drive.

The idea is that by placing the shuttle farther into your opponent's court, it becomes more difficult for them to play a tight, tumbling net shot. The precision of a net shot played from the distance would likely be low, and a tumbling shuttle would stabilize by the time it passes over the net. As a result, this type of neutral shot makes any reply close to the net less effective and even risky, because that would actually create the opportunity to play a tight, tumbling net shot in return. Even taking the shuttle slightly below the net tape is usually still an advantage, as long as it is stable and close enough to the net to either play a tight, tumbling net shot or flick the shuttle to the rear court at the last moment.

In a nutshell, by playing a flat neutral shot, you effectively take away the front court (for both players), making the court feel smaller and limiting your opponent's attacking options. Your opponent may drive back, play a flat lift, or simply reply with another neutral shot—all of which are manageable.

This type of neutral shot can be played from anywhere between the baseline and the front service line. When playing it from the middle or from closer to the front service line, it is common to avoid the sidelines and instead aim slightly toward the center to reduce your opponent's attacking angles. This allows you to cover flat replies to the rear court more easily.

Typical attacking shots that can be neutralized using this type of neutral shot are:

  • flat lift or push
  • smash (especially if your opponent follows up quickly and you do not manage to defend to the rear court)*
  • attacking clear (if not intercepted by you)
  • drive (which can be either attacking or neutral depending on the situation)

Side note: If your opponent smashes while out of position and recovers late, your return can be more aggressive by playing it closer to the net.

Example: If you are forced deep into your backhand rear corner and play a slow, tight net shot, your opponent will likely reach it early and either kill it or gain the advantage with a tight net reply. Instead, playing a fast, controlled shot over the net and deeper into the court limits their options and keeps you in the rally.

High lifts and clears

Apart from neutralizing shots flat over the net as explained above, any high clear or lift to the baseline is essentially a neutral or near-neutral shot, as smashing from the baseline is not ideal, especially if the shuttle is falling straight down. If your opponent plays a tight, tumbling net shot and you manage to lift high and all the way to the baseline, you effectively neutralize the situation. Similarly, if you are caught deep in the rear court and play a clear to the baseline, you are usually out of trouble.

Down-the-Line vs Cross-Court

Both down-the-line and cross-court shots can put your opponent under pressure, but in different ways.

Trade-offs

Down-the-line shots are fast, often easier to execute, do not pass through the central area of the court, and may be difficult to return into open space (cross-court). This makes them dangerous, but also predictable, so your opponent usually covers down-the-line shots by adjusting their base position toward that side, as discussed in the chapter on the base position.

Playing cross-court can surprise your opponent and opens up the court, making both players cover more distance. Let's say you play a cross-court clear. That means your opponent has to move a greater distance from their adjusted base position than if you played a down-the-line clear. Nevertheless, you also have to move all the way to the other side of your court to adjust your own base position, too. However, cross-court lifts and clears must be high enough to avoid being intercepted.

Down-the-line vs cross-court in defense

For certain shots, it is advisable to reply down-the-line, and for others, cross-court. If your opponent plays cross-court, they may sometimes not have enough time to be perfectly positioned to cover your down-the-line reply. That may be the case if your opponent's cross-court shot is not a clear, but a fast downward shot from the rear court, like a half-smash or smash. In that situation a straight reply can be very effective, even if anticipated. After a half-smash there is usually only time for one fast, large step to recover. Full-power smashes are often followed up toward the net immediately without taking a split step or base position in between. If your opponent attacks cross-court from the rear court, they will often not be fully recovered from the corner. In this situation, a straight reply to the front court is effective. This results in a fast return while forcing your opponent to cover the full diagonal of the court. However, similar to the clear scenario mentioned above, you also have to stretch more to retrieve that cross-court shot from your own adjusted base position in the first place.

Down-the-line smashes, on the other hand, are usually best defended cross-court or toward the center. Otherwise, unless you defend by lifting to the rear court, your opponent might get a chance to kill your straight return at the net. If you don't manage to return the smash cross-court, it should be placed farther into your opponent's court, as discussed in the subchapter on neutral shots. To sum it up, an effective way to defend smashes is to return them away from your opponent. That means, most of the time, cross-court smashes should be returned down-the-line, while down-the-line smashes should be returned cross-court.

Defensive Strategies

There are two main defensive styles.

Expanding the court (movement-based defense)

The previous subchapter discusses how playing cross-court moves both players, opening up the court and making it feel larger for both players. You can use that to your advantage if you prefer a more movement-intensive defensive style. To do this, you should mainly focus on playing high to the rear court and into open spaces. Once your opponent is slightly out of position in the rear court or toward the sides, you can start playing closer to the net, gradually putting them out of position.

However, that is not the only way of playing defensively. There is another, almost opposite approach that is still defensive.

Shrinking the court (control-based defense)

You do not have to rely on high or cross-court shots to make the game physically demanding. Instead, you can adopt a more passive style by relying on neutral shots. This style uses soft, controlled, flat shots that avoid the net area, often played down-the-line or, when not hit from the rear court, toward the center. By taking away the net area, and playing a lot down-the-line and toward the center, you are essentially "shrinking" the court for both players. Your opponent does not have to cover much distance—and neither do you. However, you make the rallies long and grinding overall, by denying your opponent angles and attacking opportunities. If your opponent loses patience and tries to attack from an unfavorable position, you can counter-attack.

You can still play tight, tumbling net shots if your opponent gives you the opportunity, but you do not actively initiate that kind of net play. Apart from that, you should still play shots to the rear court, either high or, if your opponent does not cover it, flat.

In summary, the first strategy uses space to physically exhaust your opponent by forcing them to cover large distances with each shot, while the second limits their attacking options, creates long, physically demanding rallies, and opens up counter-attacking opportunities.
