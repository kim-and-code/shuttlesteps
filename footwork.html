Footwork

This chapter covers badminton footwork techniques, with a focus on singles. It begins with general movement principles, then moves on to specific patterns for covering each corner of the court.

Principles

Badminton footwork follows a consistent rhythm structured as a repeating cycle:

  1. Adopt a ready posture and use a split step to initiate movement from the base position to a corner
  2. Move to the corner
  3. Perform a lunge or jump combined with the hitting action
  4. Recover to the (new) base position

…and repeat

These steps, as well as the overall rhythm, are explained in this subchapter.

Ready Posture

The ready posture allows you to:

  • stay balanced
  • react quickly with both your movement and your racket
  • push off explosively

From the ground up, the ready posture can be described as follows:

  • Stay on the balls of your feet, keeping your heels slightly off the ground
  • Maintain a wide stance
  • Keep your knees bent to lower your center of gravity
  • Push your hips slightly back
  • Lean slightly forward
  • Position your non-racket arm to the side or slightly behind for balance
  • Hold your racket in front of your body with a relaxed grip and a bent elbow

You might remember from school how a force vector can be split into horizontal and vertical components. When pushing off your feet, the same idea applies. For efficient movement, the horizontal component should be as large as possible*.

Side note: In sideways jumps, this role is often split between the legs: the leg opposite to the direction of movement primarily generates horizontal drive, while the other contributes more to vertical lift.

This is why a wide stance and a low body position are important. They allow you to move more efficiently, maintain balance, and react more quickly to fast, downward shots.

Initiating Movement

Base Position

Base position refers to where you should be on the court when your opponent hits the shuttle. In other words, it is the position from which you initiate your movement. The base position is not fixed, but should be adjusted to the situation. While it is often near the center of the court, there are important nuances and exceptions.

Why not always start exactly from the center? After all, from there, every corner can be reached in one to three steps.

The reason is that the base position should reflect the likelihood and threat of your opponent's shot options. Depending on where your opponent is hitting from, some shots are more likely, easier to execute, and more dangerous than others.

For example, consider a defensive lift or clear to either rear corner that allows your opponent to attack. Compared to a cross-court smash, a down-the-line smash:

  • is often easier to execute
  • travels a shorter distance
  • stays well outside your reach if you remain in the center
  • is typically more difficult to return effectively (it often requires a cross-court reply, whereas a cross-court smash can be blocked straight; see Down-the-line vs cross-court in defense)

To account for this, your base position should shift slightly toward the side you played to, prioritizing the more likely and more dangerous options. In general, the base position depends on your opponent's available shot options, which are largely determined by your previous shot and how early your opponent can take it. In other words, your shot choice influences how you should recover.

Returning to the lift/clear example: if your shot is short, your opponent can attack with more pressure. In this case, you should position yourself slightly further back to gain more time to react.

The same principle applies to other situations. After playing a tight spinning net shot, you should stay closer to the net to cover potential net replies. If your opponent lifts, you will still have enough time to move back. After a neutral push, a more central base position is appropriate.

As mentioned earlier, efficient court coverage requires both the correct base position and an appropriate angle of the split step. The next section builds on this by looking at the split step in more detail.

Split Step

Whichever corner you are moving to, the first step should be the split step. It initiates movement by propelling you toward the shuttle. As such, the split step is one of the most fundamental elements of badminton footwork.

How to perform the split step

While the split step can be performed as a small jump, the smoothest and most efficient method is to just allow your body to drop while quickly widening your stance. Upon landing, the leg opposite to the intended direction of movement should bear most of the weight, so you can push off explosively.

Timing

The timing of the split step is crucial. If you are late, you lose valuable time reaching the shuttle. If you are too early, you risk being wrong-footed by delayed or disguised shots. One of the worst scenarios is having to change direction—essentially “starting twice”—after committing too early. This leads to several disadvantages:

  • delayed movement in the correct direction
  • covering extra distance due to initial movement in the wrong direction, requiring larger or additional steps
  • additional effort required to change direction
  • an unbalanced and inefficient stance when performing the "second start" (correctional split step)

So, how to ensure the timing is spot on? When should you let yourself drop and when should you land your split step? Landing your split step the moment your opponent's racket hits the shuttle would usually be too early. The direction of the shot only becomes clear shortly after contact; the shuttle has to actually travel a bit for you to see where it is going. By the time you land, you should already know where to move. A rough guideline is to start dropping your body as your opponent's racket makes contact with the shuttle, so that your feet land shortly after. This allows you to react more accurately and move without hesitation.

A well-timed split step is the foundation of efficient, smooth, and explosive footwork.

Variations of the split step

The most basic form starts from a stationary ready position with both feet on the ground:

  1. Adopt a ready posture
  2. Drop and widen your stance
  3. Firmly push off the ground to initiate movement (using the leg opposite to the intended direction of movement)

In most situations, however, the split step is performed while standing on one leg. That is because the split step is actually not only the first step of movement to a corner, but also the last step when recovering from a corner. Rather than returning fully to the base position, waiting, and then performing the split step, the final step of recovery from the previous corner transitions smoothly into the split step. In other words, you use the split step to transition from the recovery movement into the next movement toward the shuttle. This means the split step usually starts from one leg, with the other leg sliding into the split from the front, back, or side, depending on the situation.

Another key variable is the angle of the split step. Depending on the situation, the straight line between your feet can be:

  • parallel to the net
  • angled to the left
  • angled to the right
  • perpendicular to the net

Movement is typically fastest along this line, making the angle an important factor in efficient court coverage.

An effective way to practice this is to perform split steps from one leg while moving forward, backward, or sideways, and to combine this with different angles.

Rhythm

As mentioned earlier, badminton footwork follows a repeating cycle. It is important to note that movement speed is neither uniform nor random; it follows a controlled rhythm of acceleration and deceleration.

In general, you should try to reach the shuttle as early as possible (this does not necessarily mean hitting it immediately; see Applying Pressure). Unless your opponent plays a very high or short lift, this means moving toward the shuttle as quickly as possible. From a central base position, most shots can be reached in two steps, sometimes fewer, sometimes more. Typically, this involves a quick first step followed by a lunge or jump. If needed, the first step can be longer, or additional steps can be used.

When recovering after your shot, you should move out of the corner explosively and then gradually slow down as you approach your base position. This is more effective than starting slowly and having to speed up later to arrive on time. Not only can this make you late, but it can also disrupt your balance when entering the split step. To be stable and ready to move in any direction, you should minimize your momentum when your opponent hits the shuttle*. Slow down, adopt a ready posture, stay focused, and be prepared for the next shot. As explained earlier, your final recovery step should transition smoothly into the split step by sliding your non-weight-bearing foot into position.

Side note: Even if you are out of position when your opponent hits, it is better to stay balanced and prepared where you are than to rush into a better position at the last moment.

General Footwork Patterns

Most footwork outside of split steps, lunges, and jumps consists of combinations of the following movements:

  • running forward
  • sidesteps (chassé steps)
  • front crossovers
  • back crossovers
  • hopping twice on the same foot

These patterns are used both in the specific footwork sequences described in the next chapter and as filler steps when additional movement is required. This may occur if you are out of position, if your opponent anticipates your shot and reaches the shuttle early, or if the shuttle is deflected by the net.
